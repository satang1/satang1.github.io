The following post is a self-reflection post that was written after considering the second question in from <a href="https://www.demi.fi/keskustelu/gallupit-50-thought-provoking-questions-about-life"> here</a>.

	What makes you want to do better?

Whether it’s appearances, relationships, or abilities, everyone has their own insecurities. Whenever I browse the web or scroll down my social media page, I see these amazing people: beautiful, smart, successful, happy. <i> Am I happy? </i> 

Comparing yourself to others is unavoidable, especially in today’s world when technology has allowed us to be so interconnected. We see Victoria Secret models who are six feet tall, skinny, and gorgeous. We see brilliant prodigies, not even seven years old, playing Mozart on the piano.  And what about those self-made millionaires who seem to have it all?

Now, as Roosevelt has said, “Comparison is the thief of joy”, and I totally agree. Comparing yourself with someone you believe is “better” than you is often impractical. What use is it? If you compare yourself to others, is that going to turn you into them? No. Maybe some of us like to indulge in the fantasy. By comparing ourselves, we wonder, “What would life be like if I had . . . ?”. We like to live vicariously through our imaginations, but why subject ourselves to such cruelty? Perhaps we like the fantasy, or maybe it serves as a motivation. 

Comparing yourself to your peers can be a method for self-improvement. You study harder because you realize you want to attain a better grade. You strive to eat healthier or get stronger because you want to look like this specific fitness model (or just to improve your personal health). Therefore, conscious comparison is a powerful motivator. It makes me want to do better because I know that I can. I may not be as smart or as beautiful, I may not be as athletic or as successful, but I will (and can) be a much better version of myself.

Though I have noticed that the ones who are the closest to you, are the ones that have the most impact (i.e the ones we compare ourselves to the most). You realize that he or she is just like you, but at the same time, this friend seems to have it better in life. This can cause jealousy, frustration, and even ruin relationships. But it doesn't have to. Instead, consider the attributes that you admire about others as a goal to attain. It’s a, “If he/she can do it, so can I” kind of thinking.

And perhaps this may be an unpopular opinion, but comparison <i> can </i> be good. Though I don't think that it's something that we should be too caught up in, because constantly mulling over something that you don't have can be detrimental to individual and emotional health. Therefore, treat simple comparisons as a catalyst/motivator, and not an inhibitor for personal growth.
