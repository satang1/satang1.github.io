The following reflection is a response to a journal response for my Summer 2019 Experiential Learning course: 

	How would you describe the prominent cultural norms of your internship
	workplace and city? To start, use a description of chronemics, which is
	the study of different cultures’ use of time and prominent nonverbal
	communications, to begin exploring the prevailing norms and practices of 
	the people you work and live with in your internship city. How is the 
	culture similar to your hometown? How is it different? What are other 
	prevailing norms and practices of the people you work and live with in 
	your internship city? How is the culture different from the culture 
	found in your hometown, or at another workplace where you worked? How 
	might it be similar? Items you could consider include work schedules, 
	breaks, communications between co-workers and with supervisors, your 
	place as an intern in the organizational hierarchy, etc.


<b>Note:</b> This post assumes familiarity with Bolman & Deal's <i> Reframing Organizations</i> textbook. 

Working in a government-funded/bureaucratic company in Hong Kong, I've noticed that people at my workplace maintain a combination of monochromic time and polychromic time. On one hand, (i.e. monochromic), deadlines and tasks are fixed, and people work way past their (9 to 5) work hours. I've seen/noticed people answering/replying to emails at 2 am in the morning or bringing home any leftover tasks from work. This kind of mindset is perhaps a part of the hard-working culture of Hong Kong, but it can also be because people are dedicated to their jobs.

On the other hand (i.e. polychromic), I realized that workers tend to multi-task and complete/focus on many activities at once. Meetings and schedules (especially for those between higher/more senior colleagues), can generally be more lax and flexible (i.e. start and end times are negotiable and people are often 10-15 min late). There is an allocated time for lunch and break in which I see people interact and build strategic (i.e. long-term ) and personal relationships with their coworkers or boss. Most of the time, people don't really discuss their work or any bureaucratic concerns, but rather concentrate on learning more about the other person or discussing weekend plans/activities. In fact, on Friday nights, and on the weekends, many co-workers spend time at LKF (a popular drinking/partying/clubbing area in downtown) and let loose after a hard week of work. 

If I were to compare my current workplace culture with that of the US, I would say there are similarities in the work ethic and relaxation/socializing methods, but there are also some differences in terms of communications between superiors and workers. In the US, (depending on where you work and the industry you're in), people tend to dress more casually and also interact more with their superiors. The conversations and contributions of a worker or an intern is perhaps more involved and consistent with a full-time worker (and ideas from younger workers are more considered). But in Hong Kong, the dress code is more formal (i.e. at least smart casual), and there is a need to be more formal/polite/respectful with co-workers or people with more seniority. However, this distance/hierarchy is less important as you establish yourself over time, and eventually individual/newer workers will have more input/contributions.
