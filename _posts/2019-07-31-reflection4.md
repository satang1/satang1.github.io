The following reflection is a response to a journal response for my Summer 2019 Experiential Learning course: 

	1. Think about the challenges facing leaders. What are the attributes of 
	leaders who can effectively draw on all of the four frames?

	2. Write a 300- to 450-word response that addresses all of these 
	questions:

	Describe someone you know (or knew) as an effective leader, and explain 
	why you think he/she was effective. You can draw your example from your 
	personal experience with leaders, including coaches, teachers, clergy, 
	work or internship supervisors, etc. Be sure to describe how their 
	leadership drew from elements of different frames when possible.

	After reading the assigned pieces on leaders in the corporate, 
	government, and nonprofit sectors, do you think that leaders in the
	different sectors need to have particular personal attributes and skill 
	sets for their particular career sector and, accordingly, adopt certain 
	frame preferences? If so, provide examples; if not, explain why not.


<b>Note:</b> This post assumes familiarity with Bolman & Deal's <i> Reframing Organizations</i> textbook. 

There are several challenges that a leader may encounter, including how to deal with potential employee conflicts, organizing/handling team relationships, and of course, being able to handle the pressure of making important/pivotal decisions for an organization. These kinds of challenges require that an effective leader be charismatic and communicative to ensure structural and symbolic organization (i.e. align, inspire, and remind others of core values). He/She must also be humane and compassionate so that team values and the personal needs of employees are addressed. This is also important because "[a]uthority and leadership are both built on voluntary compliance" (Bolman & Deal p. 333). Therefore, if a leader is unable to be supportive and concerned for his/her workers, they will lose the ability to lead. Being able to understand and emphasize cooperation/compromise is also important in resolving political conflicts. 

When I was in my school's dragon boat team (i.e. a water sport team), we had a group leader responsible for our everyday conditioning workout. She was one of the most encouraging and effective leaders I knew, because not only did she push us to be the best that we could be (i.e. doing more challenging workouts), she also understood our limitations and catered to any concerns that we had (either personal issues or issues within the team). As such, she employed a very effective human resource frame by valuing our opinions and personal needs. Moreover, despite the friendly relationship that we had, she made sure to reinforce the fact that there was a sort of hierarchy within the group. In this way, she employed a structural frame by ensuring that there was organization and rules that needed to be followed (i.e. meet up at 4pm, warm-ups first, follow the workout instructions, safety rules, etc.). This was effective in maintaining order, and in making sure that we were the most productive with our time since we didn't have to spend time deciding the workout routine everyday or where to meet up. Of course, as the leader of our conditioning group she was also able to employ a political frame in resolving any conflicts or tensions while we worked out. That is, if someone was holding the team up or had accidentally injured a teammate, she would be the first to respond and determine how to mitigate emotional tensions.
 
I think that leaders in different sectors do need to have different attributes and employ/adapt certain frames in order to lead effectively. If we consider corporate sectors, there is more of a focus on a structural or even human resources frame because leaders will need to be able to communicate hierarchical authority and/or consider their employees' needs. For instance, if we look at Karren Brady's case, she was able succeed because she was "opinionated about putting her staff first" (p. 2). This allowed her to build a team that would support her authority and help her manage the football team. On the other hand, for non-profit groups, it might be more important to have vision/charisma (symbolic frame), because, as stated in "Wanted Nonprofit Leaders", there will be "constant fundraising, and strategies for social change are much more complicated...[there's] a lot of burnout"(p. 2). Therefore, it is important for a leader to be able to motivate and direct its people/workers to work towards a common goal. Lastly, in a political/government organization, it might be more important to employ a political frame in order to agenda-plan for any adversaries for the future. The frame is also important in understanding how to resolve conflicts between employees/workers. Thus, I would say that in different situations, there is a need to use/employ different frames and effective leaders will need to adopt different attributes to address these scenarios.
