The photograph shown in Fig. 1 will be used to demonstrate the urban space and daily life of the people living in Hong Kong. Throughout this report, I will be inputting my personal thoughts, reflections, and experiences as a supplement to my observations and analysis. First, I will analyze how this photograph is relevant in representing Hong Kong’s role as an “in-between” for the Eastern and Western parts of the world. Then, I will transition into explaining how this picture illustrates the integration of immigrants and how they have been historically represented in popular media. Finally, I will illustrate the role that popular culture has in shaping everyday behavior and Hong Kong’s cultural identity.

If we take a look at the photograph, it is obvious that there are a lot of people in the MTR station. While this phenomenon is simply a part of everyday life in Hong Kong, this is rarely seen in the United States. However, what’s interesting about the photograph is that it illustrates how Hong Kong serves as a unique platform bridging the East and the West. I was able to see people of different ethnic backgrounds much more frequently than I had originally imagined, especially in Asia. And what’s surprising is how comfortable the local people are with adjusting to these different faces. That is, you can see foreigners in the picture, but no one stops to stare or tries to avoid these visitors. Rather, people are comfortable with this integration, and locals consider the presence of these foreigners as everyday experiences. From this, I am reminded of Hong Kong’s history as a British colony, and the influences that have contributed to Hong Kong’s depiction as a global city. However, the presence of international visitors and historical influences from the West does not strip away any of the local elements (e.g. the Cantonese language, Cantopop, TVB dramas). In fact, “Hong Kong is more Chinese than China” (Chan 2007 p. 379), but also distinct in that it welcomes ideas and perspectives that aligned with western ideals. This “in-between-ness” is apparent in the types of fusion foods available, the language, the double-decker buses, and, of course, local interaction with foreign faces.

Taking this further, a subtlety of the photo is how immigrants from other parts of Asia have integrated themselves into Hong Kong culture. At first glance, it may be hard to identify those who have come from the mainland or those who may have immigrated from other Asian countries. But within the large crowd, I could clearly hear different languages and dialects, indicating that they may not be local Hong Kongers. Hearing this, I’m reminded of our discussion on the classic Hong Kong TV series, “The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly”. In his analysis of the television series, Eric Kit-wai Ma points out that Ah Chian, the “outsider” or mainlander immigrant of the show, strives to “shake of the stigma of a low-life immigrant, [and] aspires to be a Hong Kong Man like all others” (Ma, E.K.W. 1999 p. 68). In the same way, I’ve realized that quite a few mainlanders have also attempted to assimilate to the Hong Kong culture in order to fit in. In fact, some of them even spoke Cantonese (albeit with a little bit of an accent). This phenomenon is also prevalent in my workplace, where many Chinese mainlanders or Indians have learned to speak the language and integrated themselves into Hong Kong society. So, reflecting upon these considerations, a photograph of a crowded MTR station reveals so much more than the everyday transportation experience. Instead, there is a hidden depth to this photograph because within this crowd, there may be immigrants who have adapted to the culture and lifestyle of Hong Kong in order to shake off any perception of being an “outsider”. 
Considering this, it would be appropriate to further analyze the role that social media and popular culture has on the cultural identity in Hong Kong.

If we take a look at the photograph, you’ll notice that a lot of people are on their phones, either watching shows, listening to music, or surfing the web. As such, it is obvious how prevalent and influential popular culture can be on the thoughts and processes of young people. Like the portrayal of mainlanders in “The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly”, certain groups can be forced into a specific set of stereotypes just by the way they are presented in the media. And as argued in the paper, “Outsiders on Television”, collective sentiments can “[influence] the way in which people see themselves and the ways in which they relate to [these outsiders]. (Ma, E.K.W. 1999 p. 80). Therefore, it is obvious that popular culture, including songs, actors (e.g. Andy Lau, Tony Leung), city streets, or even cinematic films (e.g. “In a Mood for Love”), has a large role in cultivating Hong Kong’s identity, attitude and even its collective memory. In fact, this photograph also documents a sort of collective memory, reminding people of the bustling culture of the city. That is, the crowds, the people, the voices, and the experiences of everyday transportation – all of this identifies and reminisces Hong Kong’s urban scene and fast-paced lifestyle.

Overall, I think that this picture not only illustrates the typical experience of living in a region like Hong Kong, but it also points out the nuances of experiencing a culture that has been influenced by its history. That is, Hong Kong is “…the legacy of Chinese values hybridized with … British imposed rituals and norms.” (Eric K. W. Ma & Anthony Y. H. Fung 2007 p. 173). Therefore, this photograph is not only an observation of the everyday experience, but also the culmination of culture, history, and Hong Kong people’s individual values.
