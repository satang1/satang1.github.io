The following reflection is a response to a journal response for my Summer 2019 Experiential Learning course: 

	1. Read "Monarchs, Lords, and Serfs" and answer the questions listed.

	Do you think the piece captures some of the nuances involving the roles 
	of leaders, middle managers, and line staff in your organization, or in 
	another organization you are familiar with? If so, how? If not, why not?

	Do you think there are cultural issues/ workplace norms in your workplace 
	that are different from those you would expect in workplaces in your 
	hometown?  If so, how?  If not, why not?  (In answering this last 
	question you may want to review and further reflect upon the information 
	on ‘Chronemics’ presented in Module 2.)


<b>Note:</b> This post assumes familiarity with Bolman & Deal's <i> Reframing Organizations</i> textbook, as well as the "Monarchs, Lords, and Serfs" reading. 

If I were to compare these insights to the roles of leaders and employees in my current organization, I can say it certainly does capture the nuances of the hierarchical structure in my workplace. Since my company is funded by the government, it reinforces a very top-down structure in which the CEOs and top executives act as "monarchs" overseeing the work and decisions of the working "serfs" or office workers. The piece outlines describes "monarchs" being bombarded with a lot of responsibility, and I can see how this can be comparable to the company executives at my workplace. A lot of times, I see executives rushing to handle immediate situations (i.e. press conference releases, public services, etc.), which can allow "urgent problems to pile up" ('Monarchs, Lords, and Serfs" p. 2).  On the other hand, the middle managers (i.e the department managers/supervisors I report to) can be seen aggregating or implementing the many sources of power that were listed in Bolman and Deal's chapter on the political structure. For instance, many department managers can be seen mingling with other people who have connections or "position power (authority)" (p. 223), because as was described, "the key difference between more and less successful senior managers [is] attentiveness to building and cultivating ties with friends and allies" (p. 193). This makes it easier for these "lords" to accomplish their tasks and to make their boss happy. Likewise, in a hierarchal/feudal-like/bureaucratic-like system, the workers (aka. interns and blue-collar employees) are (as always) responsible for most of the heavy lifting/labor. But perhaps a difference is that workers nowadays enjoy more personal rights, have more standardized working hours and receive more respect for their work.

I also think that there are differences between the cultural norms in my Hong Kong workplace and how I would describe a workplace in the States. That is, it is common for people in HK to work overtime and to handle multiple tasks at the same time.  Perhaps this is because of a culture that ingrains hard work and supports multi-tasking to accomplish more tasks. Regardless, this polychromic concentration is in contrast to earlier discussions in the textbook describing how structural perspectives originated from ideas of "designing organizations for maximum efficiency" (p. 48). In fact, Frederick Taylor described how "[breaking] tasks into minute parts" (p. 48) could boost efficiency and emphasizes a "one-task focus". The emphasis on concentrating on one task at a time is perhaps a difference in the sense of time. Communicating proficiency and ability in the States can mean completing a single project well (even if it might take longer), but in Hong Kong, they seem to value completing more tasks within a shorter amount of time. Moreover, the States values individualism and independence more than in HK, where community, connections, and relationships are more valuable for professional and personal stability/benefits.
